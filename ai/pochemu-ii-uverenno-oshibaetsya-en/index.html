Why AI Gets Things Wrong with Confidence: The Nature of Hallucinations

A language model can invent a non-existent book, court case, or code function — and do it in a flawlessly confident tone. This is not a malfunction but a direct consequence of how the model is built. We break down where hallucinations come from and how to live with them.

Editorial20 июня 2026 г.3 минуты чтения

In 2023, an American lawyer filed a court document citing half a dozen legal precedents. Not one of them existed: all the cases had been invented by a chatbot, and the lawyer did not check. The story became a textbook example, but its main point is often missed — the model did not break and did not "lie". It did exactly what it was built to do.

Plausibility instead of truth

A language model is trained to continue text so that the continuation is statistically similar to the texts in its training data. The name of a court case looks like "Ivanov v. Company N, 2019"; a bibliographic reference looks like "Author, 'Title', publisher, year". The model has learned the form perfectly — and it faithfully reproduces it, even when the specific content is nowhere in its parameters.

In this sense, a hallucination is not a deviation from normal operation but its limiting case: the query leads the model into territory where data was scarce, yet it still knows how to answer in the learned form.

The model has no internal boundary between "I remember" and "I'm making this up smoothly". Both modes are one and the same operation: choosing the most plausible continuation.

Where hallucinations live most often

In practice, it's fairly predictable. The risk is highest where the answer needs to be a precise fact rather than reasoning:

  • exact figures, dates, "verbatim" quotes;
  • references to sources: books, articles, laws, court cases;
  • rare entities — little-known people, niche libraries, recent events;
  • junctions of languages and cultures: a question in Russian about something described mostly in English-language data.

And conversely: retelling common knowledge, structuring your own text, drafting an email — these are areas where the model rarely errs, because it doesn't have to "recall" anything.

How the industry deals with it — and what you can take for yourself

The industry attacks the problem from several directions: models are connected to search engines and document databases so that answers rest on retrieved text rather than memory (the RAG approach — retrieval-augmented generation); they are fine-tuned to answer "I don't know"; responses are run through verifier models. It helps, but it doesn't solve the problem: a generator of plausible text remains a generator of plausible text.

The user-level rules are simpler:

  1. Anything that looks like a precise fact gets verified against an external source. Always.
  2. Ask the model to cite sources — and open them: a non-existent reference exposes a hallucination instantly.
  3. Phrase your query so the model works with your material ("here's the text — find the contradictions") rather than pulling facts from memory.
  4. A confident tone means nothing. The model reports the multiplication table and a fabricated biography with equal confidence.

Hallucinations are the price we pay for the versatility of generative models. That doesn't make the tool useless — it makes it a tool that demands skill. Like almost everything worthwhile.
