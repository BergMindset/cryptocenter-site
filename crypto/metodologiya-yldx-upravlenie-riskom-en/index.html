Risk in DeFi is not a lottery — it's math: the YLDX methodology

In 2022, $3.2 billion was stolen from DeFi — almost 6% of the entire market. Yet the risk of a liquidity pool can be calculated in advance and spread out so that the failure of a single pool turns from a catastrophe into a scratch. Here is the methodology the YLDX fund uses to pick pools, allocate capital and cap losses.

Editorial13 июля 2026 г.6 минут чтения

People love to talk loudly about DeFi yields and quietly about DeFi risk. We believe an honest conversation starts in the opposite order: first — how much you can lose and with what probability, and only then — how much you can earn. Below is the methodology behind the YLDX indices (Stable, Coin, DEX): not a promise of profit, but a set of rules that limit the damage.

What risk actually “costs”

Let's start with numbers that are rarely put side by side.

  • DeFi capitalisation at the end of 2022 — about $54 billion of total value locked (TVL).
  • Over the same year, $3.2 billion was stolen through hacks and fraudulent projects — roughly 5.9% of the entire sector.
  • 59% of those losses came from attacks on cross-chain bridges — not on liquidity pools themselves.

The first conclusion follows immediately: a large share of systemic risk in DeFi lives not in pools but in the bridges between chains. A pool that depends on a bridge, by definition, carries both risks at once.

To estimate the risk of a single pool, take a rough but honest benchmark. The sector's average TVL between peak and trough is ($324B + $120B) / 2 ≈ $222B. Dividing the year's losses by that base gives a reference “loss share” per unit of capital in the sector — about 3.44% per year. This is not the probability that your specific pool gets hacked tomorrow; it is a coarse ceiling: that is how much, on average, “burns” per unit of invested capital across the whole market in a year.

Nobody knows the exact probability of a single pool being hacked. But knowing the order of magnitude is already enough to build a defence.

Diversification turns a failure into a scratch

From here, simple arithmetic takes over. If no pool holds more than 2.5% of the portfolio, the total loss of one pool costs at most 2.5% of capital — and since pools rarely go all the way to zero, the expected annual hit from any single pool is measured in fractions of a percent.

Take a conservative scenario. Spread the capital across 100+ pools, with each pool's share capped. Even if you apply the sector-wide 3.44% loss ceiling to every pool, the contribution of one pool to the total loss is:

2.5% (pool share) × 3.44% (loss ceiling) ≈ 0.086% of the portfolio.

In other words: an adverse event in one pool costs the investor less than one tenth of a percent of capital. That — not a “pretty number of pools” but a hard cap on the maximum single hit — is what broad diversification is for.

Three indices — three risk profiles

One portfolio should not carry one single risk. So the capital is split across three indices with different sources of yield and different depths of diversification.

The allocation logic: the higher the volatility and the potential yield, the wider the diversification required to keep risk in check. Stablecoin pools barely know impermanent loss — but there are many of them, to spread individual protocol risk. DEX pools promise more — and demand the most careful slicing.

Seven rules for entering and exiting a pool

The methodology is not “buy and hold” — it is a set of hard constraints, identical across all three indices.

  1. Per-pool cap — 2.5%. No pool ever receives more than 2.5% of total assets. This is the upper bound of damage from a single point of failure.
  2. Liquidity threshold — TVL > $1M. Only pools with TVL above one million dollars make it into an index. Small, unstable pools are cut off: they are easier to manipulate and drain.
  3. Lending-protocol limit — 20%. No more than 20% of deposits go into lending protocols. This caps cascade risk in a market crash.
  4. Enter the most profitable. Priority goes to the highest-yielding pools among those selected for the index — subject to every constraint above.
  5. Exit the least profitable. Withdrawals come first from the index's least profitable pools, preserving the portfolio structure.
  6. Fees no higher than 0.1%. Assets are moved only if gas stays below 0.1% of the amount. Expensive transactions are simply not executed.
  7. Impermanent loss under control. The share of pools exposed to impermanent loss is capped at 25%. Exits from such pools are deferred and positions are held for at least 14 months — so that compounding fees outgrow the impermanent loss.

The seventh rule is the one newcomers miss most often: an impermanent loss only becomes real at exit. While the position is held and earning fees, time works for the investor, not against them.

What these numbers mean — and what they don't

Honestly, about the model's limits:

  • Target percentages are reference points, not a promise. The market can deliver less — and in a bad stretch, take the result negative. No diversification cancels market risk.
  • The 3.44% risk estimate is a coarse ceiling derived from sector statistics, not the exact probability for your pool. It is there for the order of magnitude, not for prediction.
  • The source of truth is on-chain and the Center. Index composition, TVL and yields are reconciled against protocol data; wherever signals diverge, the blockchain wins. You can check the live index composition and the assets of any wallet right here, in the wallet explorer and on the Pulse page.

The point of the whole methodology is not to promise a lot, but to make sure that no single mistake, hack or crash ever costs the investor more than a pre-computed share. Risk cannot be removed. It can be measured, capped and spread.

This is educational material about a methodology, not financial advice. Past performance and target figures do not guarantee future results.
